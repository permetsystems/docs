# Overview

## Mission

Build an open-source, end-to-end process for fully transparent, participant-owned apparel production.

## Goals

1. **Participant-owned apparel** — Community-curated collections where contributors share ownership of the upside, not just wages.
2. **Fashion Legos** — A forkable garment library: open-source production infrastructure that lets creators build new garments on existing bases via on-chain portfolios that can be forked, remixed, and recombined.
3. **Garment creator economy** — Cryptographic profit sharing so creators and makers retain the value they create in making and wearing apparel.

## Three core concepts

### 1. Open fashion blocks

A garment library serving as an open-source, Github-like platform for apparel production — end-to-end supply chain and tooling that lets creators easily produce new garments on existing bases.

### 2. Curated registries

Token-curated registries of designers, producers, and suppliers who have successfully used the core garment library. Network effects make each new project easier by forking and building on existing on-chain portfolios of garments, materials, and designs.

### 3. Split system

Cryptographic profit sharing that retains and redistributes value created in making and wearing apparel — routing future royalties back to creators and the community.

## Why reinvention is necessary

Traditional apparel maximizes profit by minimizing value returned to stakeholders:

- **Humanitarian exploitation** — sweatshop, child, and forced labor in supply chains, with no way to verify ethical production.
- **Creative exploitation** — designers generate brand value but rarely share downstream earnings; designs become brand IP.
- **Environmental exploitation** — planned obsolescence and overconsumption block real sustainability.

Supply chain anonymity means even well-intentioned designers can be complicit in exploitation they never see.

## What the protocol enables

**Empowerment**

- Normalize ethical labor and proportional compensation.
- Onboard designers and makers onto transparent infrastructure.
- Stronger, more diverse design communities driven by aligned incentives.

**Ownership**

- Web3 communities returning more value to creators when producing wearables.
- Forkable concepts and tools validated and iterated on in the open.
- Computer-aided designs registered on-chain as vectors for cut-and-sew, print, and other production — enabling proper credit and compensation.
- Future royalties distributed to creators and community via programmatic rules.

**Better incentives**

- Shareholders, makers, and creators need not be in opposition.
- Effective products and human rights need not be in opposition.

## Returning ownership

- On-chain tools for designers to register work and ensure attribution.
- Product structures that return a percentage of ownership to buyers, enabling access to dividends.
- Community-governed treasuries supporting the communities that supply trends and raw materials.

## Roadmap

Full on-chain garment production is a large undertaking. The protocol advances through product testing bounties, community curation, cross-world infrastructure, and blockchain development — toward participant-owned brands with full on-chain supply.
